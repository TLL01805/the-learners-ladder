Author's Purpose, Tone & Point of View

Every piece of writing was created for a reason, in a particular voice, from a particular angle. Learn to detect all three, and no text can quietly slip past you unquestioned.

The Learners Ladder Editorial Team | 18 min read | Reading Comprehension

No text is neutral by accident. Every writer makes choices — why they're writing, what attitude to take, and what angle to view the subject from — and a sharp reader can detect all three, even when none of them are stated outright.

This guide closes our Reading Comprehension series by tying together its final three skills: recognizing why a text was written, identifying the tone it's written in, and detecting the point of view or bias behind it — building on the main idea, inference, context clue, and structure skills covered earlier in this series.

1.What Is Author's Purpose?

Author's purpose is the reason a piece of writing exists — what the writer is trying to achieve by producing it. Every text has at least one purpose, even when it isn't stated directly.

2.The Three Core Purposes: PIE

Most writing purposes fall into three broad categories, often remembered with the acronym PIE.

Persuade

To convince the reader of an opinion or to take a specific action. Common in opinion columns, advertisements, and speeches. "We must act now to protect our coastlines before it's too late."

Inform

To explain or provide factual information, without pushing a particular opinion. Common in textbooks, news reports, and instructions. "Coastal erosion has increased by 12% over the past decade, according to new survey data."

Entertain

To engage, amuse, or emotionally move the reader. Common in fiction, humor writing, and much poetry. "The tide crept in like an old friend who never quite remembered to leave."

A fourth, related purpose is sometimes distinguished separately: to explain a process or concept — closely related to informing, but focused specifically on how or why something works, as in a how-to guide or a scientific explanation.

3.How to Identify Author's Purpose

  • Word choice: persuasive texts often use emotionally loaded language; informative texts favor neutral, precise language.
  • Evidence type: statistics and citations suggest informing; emotional appeals and calls to action suggest persuading.
  • Structure: a problem-and-solution structure (Topic IV) often signals persuasive intent; a purely descriptive structure often signals informing.
  • Genre and context: where a text is published — a scientific journal, a novel, an opinion column — is often the strongest single clue to its purpose.

4.What Is Tone?

Tone is the author's attitude toward the subject, expressed through word choice, sentence structure, and style. It answers the question: how does the writer feel about what they are writing?

Same Topic, Different Tone

Critical tone: "The proposal is riddled with vague promises and unrealistic timelines." Optimistic tone: "The proposal offers an ambitious, exciting vision for what the team could achieve." Neutral tone: "The proposal outlines a three-year timeline for the project." Same subject, three entirely different attitudes.

5.Tone vs. Mood

These two are frequently confused, but they describe different things entirely.

TermWhose Feeling?Created By
ToneThe author's attitudeWord choice, style
MoodThe reader's emotional responseAtmosphere, setting, imagery

A writer can have a detached, clinical tone while still creating a deeply unsettling mood in the reader — the two don't always match.

6.Diction: How Word Choice Reveals Tone

Diction — a writer's specific word choices — is the primary tool for creating tone. Two sentences can describe the identical event with completely different diction and produce completely different tones.

Example

"The manager fired the employee without warning." (blunt, harsh) vs. "The manager let the employee go after a difficult decision." (softer, more sympathetic) — the same event, described with very different diction and tone.

8.What Is Point of View (in Nonfiction)?

In fiction, point of view refers to narrative perspective (first person, third person, and so on), covered in our companion Prose guide. In nonfiction and comprehension contexts, point of view refers to the author's perspective or stance on a subject — including any bias that stance might introduce.

Objective vs. Subjective Point of View

Objective: presents facts without revealing a personal opinion. "The policy reduced emissions by 15% over three years." Subjective: reveals a personal stance or judgment. "The policy's modest 15% reduction falls embarrassingly short of what's actually needed."

9.Detecting Bias

Bias is a slanted or one-sided point of view, often concealed within seemingly factual language. Recognizing it is an essential comprehension skill, especially in news, opinion writing, and advertising.

  • Loaded language: emotionally charged words used to steer the reader's reaction, rather than neutral description.
  • Selective evidence: presenting only facts that support one side, while omitting relevant context.
  • Unbalanced framing: giving significantly more space, detail, or sympathetic language to one perspective over another.
  • Unsupported generalizations: broad claims presented without evidence, especially about groups of people.
Example: Spotting Bias

"The so-called 'experts' claim the new policy will help — but ordinary people know better." The phrase "so-called," the scare quotes around "experts," and the implied conflict between experts and "ordinary people" all reveal a biased point of view, despite the sentence never stating an opinion directly.

10.How Purpose, Tone, and Point of View Work Together

These three elements are deeply connected: purpose shapes tone, and tone often reveals point of view. A persuasive purpose frequently produces a passionate or critical tone; an informative purpose more often produces a neutral, objective tone — though skilled persuasive writers sometimes deliberately adopt a neutral-sounding tone specifically to appear more objective and credible than they actually are.

Real-World Example

Consider this excerpt from an opinion piece: "City officials tout the new transit plan as 'revolutionary,' yet residents in outlying neighborhoods — conveniently absent from the glossy press materials — will see little benefit from a system that mainly serves downtown commuters." The purpose is to persuade the reader that the plan is flawed. The tone is skeptical and critical, revealed through diction like "tout" (rather than "announce") and "conveniently absent" (implying deliberate omission). The point of view is subjective and clearly biased against the plan, using selective framing — highlighting one affected group while omitting any benefits the plan might offer. Recognizing all three transforms this from "a fact about a transit plan" into "one writer's specific argument about a transit plan" — an essential distinction for any careful reader.

Why This Matters

Recognizing author's purpose, tone, and point of view is what separates passive reading from genuinely critical reading. Combined with main idea, inference, context clues, and text structure — the four skills covered earlier in this series — this final skill completes a full toolkit for approaching any text with confidence: understanding not just what it says, but why it was written, how it feels, and whose perspective it represents.

Test Your Understanding

Practice Questions

Twelve multiple-choice questions. Tap "Show Answer" under any question to check it individually, or reveal the full answer key at the end.

1.What does the acronym PIE stand for in relation to author's purpose?
  • A. Persuade, Inform, Entertain
  • B. Predict, Infer, Explain
  • C. Person, Idea, Evidence
  • D. Purpose, Intent, Effect
Answer: A — Persuade, Inform, Entertain. The three most common broad categories of author's purpose.
2.What is tone?
  • A. The reader's emotional response
  • B. The author's attitude toward the subject
  • C. The setting of a story
  • D. A type of text structure
Answer: B. Tone reflects the author's attitude, expressed through word choice and style.
3.What is the key difference between tone and mood?
  • A. There is no difference
  • B. Tone is the author's attitude; mood is the reader's emotional response
  • C. Mood only applies to poetry
  • D. Tone only applies to nonfiction
Answer: B. Tone belongs to the author; mood is the atmosphere or feeling experienced by the reader.
4.What is diction?
  • A. A grammar rule
  • B. A writer's specific word choices
  • C. The structure of a paragraph
  • D. A type of punctuation
Answer: B. Diction refers to specific word choices, which are the primary tool for creating tone.
5.Which sentence uses more objective point of view?
  • A. The policy's modest reduction falls embarrassingly short.
  • B. The policy reduced emissions by 15% over three years.
  • C. The so-called policy barely helped anyone.
  • D. Officials tout the policy as a miracle fix.
Answer: B. It states a fact without revealing personal judgment, unlike the other options.
6.What is loaded language?
  • A. Extremely long sentences
  • B. Emotionally charged words used to steer the reader's reaction
  • C. Words with multiple meanings
  • D. Technical vocabulary
Answer: B. Loaded language uses emotional charge to influence the reader, rather than neutral description.
7.Which purpose is most associated with a problem-and-solution text structure?
  • A. Entertain
  • B. Persuade
  • C. Describe only
  • D. None of the above
Answer: B — Persuade. Problem-and-solution structures often signal an attempt to convince the reader a particular solution is needed.
8.What is selective evidence?
  • A. Presenting only facts that support one side, omitting relevant context
  • B. Using footnotes correctly
  • C. Citing multiple balanced sources
  • D. Avoiding evidence altogether
Answer: A. Selective evidence is a common bias technique that presents a one-sided picture while appearing factual.
9.Why might a persuasive writer deliberately adopt a neutral-sounding tone?
  • A. To make the writing longer
  • B. To appear more objective and credible than they actually are
  • C. Neutral tone is always required by law
  • D. It has no strategic purpose
Answer: B. A neutral tone can make persuasive or biased content seem more trustworthy than a clearly opinionated tone would.
10."The manager let the employee go after a difficult decision" vs. "The manager fired the employee without warning" — what does this pair demonstrate?
  • A. Two unrelated events
  • B. How diction changes tone even when describing the same event
  • C. A change in author's purpose only
  • D. A shift in text structure
Answer: B. The same event is described with different diction, producing two very different tones.
11.In nonfiction, what does "point of view" primarily refer to?
  • A. First, second, or third person narration only
  • B. The author's perspective or stance, including possible bias
  • C. The font style used
  • D. The chronological order of events
Answer: B. In nonfiction and comprehension contexts, point of view refers to the author's stance and potential bias, distinct from narrative point of view in fiction.
12.Which clue is generally considered the strongest single indicator of a text's purpose?
  • A. The number of paragraphs
  • B. Where the text is published and its genre
  • C. The font size
  • D. The length of the title
Answer: B. Genre and publication context — a scientific journal versus an opinion column, for instance — is often the strongest single clue to purpose.
